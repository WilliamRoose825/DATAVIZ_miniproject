In this mini-project, we will be working with a data set of baby names in France. It contains the list of all baby names registered in France, year by year, from 1900 through 2020. There are two data sets: one aggregated to the national level, and another with data by department. Your goal is to create 3 different visualizations around these data, each focussed on answering different kinds of questions about the data:

Visualization 1: How do baby names evolve over time? Are there names that have consistently remained popular or unpopular? Are there some that have were suddenly or briefly popular or unpopular? Are there trends in time?
Visualization 2: Is there a regional effect in the data? Are some names more popular in some regions? Are popular names generally popular across the whole country?
Visualization 3: Are there gender effects in the data? Does popularity of names given to both sexes evolve consistently? (Note: this data set treats sex as binary; this is a simplification that carries into this assignment but does not generally hold.)

For each of your three visualizations:

- A description of why you think this solution is appropriate and effective at answering their target questions (does each question is answered clearly?).
(If you really are stuck answering all these questions in a single visualization, you may submit multiple solutions; be sure to compare and contrast them.)

Try to be clear, as concise as possible but have a great self-critique and analysis.

VISUALIZATION 1 — How do baby names evolve over time?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Screenshot A — Full chart, no selection]
The default view shows all 20 lines at once over the full 1900–2020 range. Even
without interaction, two broad patterns are immediately readable: a high-plateau era
dominated by a handful of classic names (JEAN, MARIE, PIERRE…) up to the 1960s,
then a sharp fragmentation as birth counts drop and diversity explodes.

[Screenshot B — "MICHEL" highlighted via legend click]
Clicking MICHEL in the legend dims all other lines and reveals a textbook
post-war fashion spike: near-zero before 1930, a sharp rise through the 1940s–50s
to become the top male name, then a steady collapse from the 1960s onward. This
directly answers "Are there names that were suddenly or briefly popular?"

[Screenshot C — "MARIE" highlighted]
In contrast, MARIE holds a dominant position across many decades before a long,
gradual decline. Placing it next to Screenshot B makes the difference between a
"generational fashion name" and a "century-long classic" self-evident.

Why this design works:
  • The full temporal axis (121 years) is essential — compressing it would hide
    the contrast between slow drifts and sudden spikes.
  • Binding the highlight to the legend rather than to a click on the line itself
    is deliberate: it avoids mis-clicks when lines are densely packed at the top of
    the chart, and it makes the interaction discoverable at a glance.
  • Opacity + stroke-width double-encoding during highlighting ensures the selected
    name stands out even for viewers with colour-vision deficiencies.

Self-critique:
  • The chart is fixed to the top 20 names computed over the entire period. A name
    with a brief but intense regional or generational peak that does not rank in the
    global top 20 is completely invisible.
  • The question "Are there names consistently *unpopular*?" cannot be answered at
    all: the bottom of the distribution is entirely absent from the view.
  • 20 hues are at the limit of pre-attentive perception; without the highlight
    interaction, comparing specific lines is difficult.
  • Gender is not encoded: national totals pool male and female usage, which can
    mask interesting cross-gender dynamics (explored in Viz 3).

VISUALIZATION 2 — Is there a regional effect?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Screenshot D — Year 1970, rank 1 selected (NATHALIE)]
NATHALIE fills the map in a nearly uniform mid-blue: no department deviates
dramatically from the national average (coefficient of variation ≈ 0.20, the lowest
in the dataset). This is the clearest possible visual answer to "Are popular names
generally popular across the whole country?" — at peak fashion, a dominant name
spreads uniformly across all regions.

[Screenshot E — Year 1993, rank 1 selected (KEVIN)]
KEVIN illustrates the opposite pattern to Screenshot D: a fashion name at its
peak shows strong spatial variation. The Creuse stands out in deep blue, partly
a genuine cultural effect (American-name trends penetrated rural, TV-driven areas
strongly) and partly a statistical artefact — with very few total births, a handful
of extra KEVINs sharply inflates the per-1 000 ratio. This answers "Are some names
more popular in some regions?" and also reveals a built-in limitation of proportion
encoding on low-population departments.

[Screenshot F — Year 1910, rank 2 selected (JEAN)]
For the early 20th century the colour contrast across departments is noticeably
stronger: JEAN 1910 has a coefficient of variation of 0.62, versus 0.20 for
NATHALIE 1970 (Screenshot D). Crucially, this is not just a rank-1 artefact —
ranks 2 (JEAN) and 3 (JEANNE) share the same contrasted geography (CV 0.62 and
0.50 respectively). The entire top 3 shows strong regional heterogeneity, suggesting
that first-name cultures were genuinely more localised before mass media
homogenised taste. This answers "Are some names more popular in some regions?"
with a clear temporal nuance.

Why this design works:
  • Normalising by departmental birth total (proportion per 1 000) is the key design
    decision. A raw-count map would simply mirror population density; the proportion
    map reveals genuine cultural or geographic preference.
  • The wide-pivot architecture (one column per year: p_1900 … p_2020) with a
    client-side transform_calculate selects the right column at render time, avoiding
    duplicating 95 geometry rows × 121 years in the Vega-Lite spec. This keeps the
    spec lean and the browser responsive even for a full-century slider.
  • Four DOM-TOM are repositioned as insets below the mainland, keeping them visible
    without distorting the overall map layout.
  • The auto-scaling colour domain (no fixed max) maximises geographic contrast for
    whichever year is selected, making spatial patterns pop even for years with low
    absolute counts.

Self-critique:
  • The dropdown is constrained to the three nationally top-ranked names for the
    selected year. A name with a strong regional flavour (e.g. Breton names like YANN
    or GAËLLE) that never reaches the national top 3 is completely absent.
  • Auto-scaling across years means the same hue does not represent the same
    proportion in different years, making direct cross-year comparisons misleading
    without reading the legend carefully.
  • Overseas territories are shown at an approximate scale and position; their
    distance and relative size relative to mainland France are not accurate.

VISUALIZATION 3 — Are there gender effects on unisex names?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Screenshot G — DOMINIQUE, 1950–2020, step = 5]
In the 1950s–70s, boys' and girls' bars are nearly symmetric: DOMINIQUE is
genuinely unisex. From the 1980s onward the girls' bars (right) systematically
dwarf the boys' (left). The gender flip is visible as a progressive asymmetry that
builds row by row — impossible to miss.

[Screenshot H — CAMILLE, 1900–2020, step = 5]
CAMILLE tells the opposite story to DOMINIQUE: it starts as a predominantly male
name, all but disappears in mid-century, then re-emerges from the 1980s as an
almost exclusively female name. The answer to "does popularity evolve consistently
by gender?" is here a clear no — the two sexes follow entirely opposite trajectories.
Zooming into 1980–2010 with step = 2 via the sliders further reveals the
year-by-year acceleration of feminisation, showing that the interactive controls
are not decorative: they uncover dynamics that the 5-year step would smooth over.

[Screenshot I — CLAUDE, 1920–1980, step = 3]
CLAUDE offers the counter-example: male and female counts rise and fall almost in
perfect unison (correlation = 0.99), peaking together in the 1940s–50s and declining
together afterwards. This directly answers "does popularity evolve consistently by
gender?" with a yes — when a name is culturally neutral rather than actively
reassigned, both sexes follow the same fashion cycle. Comparing CLAUDE with CAMILLE
in the same session makes the contrast between the two regimes immediately apparent.

Why this design works:
  • The butterfly layout gives boys and girls a shared baseline (x = 0), making the
    gender balance readable as a symmetry judgement — a pre-attentive task — rather
    than a numerical comparison.
  • Because both bars for a year share the same row, the total number of births
    (boys + girls combined) is directly encoded as the total width, answering a
    secondary question without extra visual elements.
  • The step slider is essential for readability: 121 yearly rows would be
    unreadable at any standard screen height; step = 5 (24 rows) is the practical
    default.

Self-critique:
  • Only names with ≥ 200 births per sex over the full period qualify as "unisex".
    A name with a notable but brief cross-gender episode (e.g. used by both sexes for
    only a decade) may be excluded.
  • Very gender-skewed names produce a near-invisible bar on one side. A viewer who
    does not notice the axis scale might conclude the minority-gender use is zero.
  • The ordinal y-axis space rows equally regardless of the step value: when
    step = 10, consecutive rows represent a decade of social change yet look
    identical in spacing to step = 1. A quantitative y-axis would encode time gaps
    honestly, at the cost of irregular bar spacing.
